    • Context

    ServiceNow is an online resource for Lexmark employees to submit requests for any items or services. Many users have not been able to quickly and accurately find request forms specific to their needs. This has resulted in delays in users’ requests being met and additional work for the Facilities team since they need to redirect the requests to the proper channels. For Summer 2019, I worked on redesigning the Facilities and Site Operations section of ServiceNow.

    Solutions at Glance

    Between the old and new design, there was a 36% overall increase in 10 common tasks success rate. Initial success rate being 53% and final success rate being 89%.

    • Usability Testing

    Because there was not a lot of data, I wanted to gather my own before embarking on prototyping redesigns. Although I would have liked to learn more about specific quantitative data such as conversion rates, bounce rates, and times being spent on individual forms to get a more accurate view of the situation, I came to a conclusion that I only had 12 weeks at Lexmark and while this was my primary project, it would not be my only one. With the limited time available, I decided to collect success rates on 10 most common tasks. I phrased these ten tasks as real life cases instead of telling them what request form to submit because that was a major confusion in the process.

      1. After work-hours, items from people’s cubicles are disappearing. The group decides to install security cameras. Please submit a request.
      2. You have lower back pain that prevents you from sitting for more than 15 minutes straight. Please submit a request for a stand-up desk.
      3. You are a manager who wants to break the wall between two small meeting rooms and make it into one medium sized meeting room. Please submit a request.
      4. A projector is broken. Please submit a request.
      5. You need to ship a 1000 lb. equipment to China. Please submit a request.
      6. You recently got injured and will need to use a wheelchair for several months. The entry to your office does not have a automotive door, please submit a request.
      7. In your office, there is a 15ft x 5ft sized whiteboard hung up. It needs to be relocated into a different room. Please submit a request.
      8. You want to move a couch out of your office. Please submit a request.
      9. A cabinet has been in your office for the last ten years. You want to get it opened, but it is locked. Please submit a request
      10. You have highly sensitive documents and you need a padlock for your door. Please submit a request.

    Here were the results of the old design

    Collecting the baseline data, not allowed me to see a clearer picture of the old design, but it also allowed me to have a standard of comparison for the success rate average of the new design later on in the study.

    Gather Pain Points

    At the end of each testing, I was able to do card sorts and ask questions about specific forms and figure out their main frustrations. The card sorts helped me see common groupings of different requests. With the quantitative data, I was able to see the most urgent confusions but the qualitative data supported by design decisions.

    Example pain points

  • When requesting a security camera,"Site Operations Security Request" seems like a great option, until you click on the form and realize it's only for location in Zhuhai, China. Come up with a design that prioritizes location first to cut out any unnecessary searhcing behaviors.
  • For an ergonomic furniture request, there is a list of options to select however, not everyone knows what each option means and options that are either/or are checkboxes which allow for multiple selections. Make the information digestible, offer categories, use radio buttons rather than checkboxes, and offer help where needed with help icons.
  • For the overall request selection process, the titles for each request were not clear or sometimes representative of the request forms. Come up with a design that has helper text for each request form.

    Design review & Revisions #1

    After my first design concept, it was time for good old design review! Design reviews were one of my favorite moments at Lexmark. Although nerve-wrecking, the reaction from my team was positive! However, they did give me advice that was crucial: forms do not always have to display all the information at first load. This got me thinking about how I can incorporate interactions that were dynamic to clean up the forms. Up until this point, I was using XD to do the mockups but decided to move to Axure for prototyping because of its capabilities for including dynamic interactions.

    Stakeholder & Developer Approval

    Showing initial prototypes on Axure made it really easy for the stakeholder to see how effective my changes would be. He was really happy about the progress! However, the developer informed me that although my three column design looked great, developers will only be able to code a two column design. A technical limitation that I did not know about was brought to light. After converting the forms to two columns from three, I was ready for another round of design review!

    Design review & Revisions #1

    For this second design review, we did a final walk through of the concept that has been revised twice since the last design review and devoted time working on strings! We learned from users that they value clarity and simplicity. With the other UX designers' advice, I decided that helper text/descriptions should be a list of services or items that can be submitted for each request form.

    Concern

    However, there were an area of concern. Goal #9 had a lower success rate this time around when compared to the old design. When people who failed the task were asked about their confusion, they stated that the wrongfully submitted form never indicated to them that they were in the wrong location. I made sure to incorporate that feedback in the last round of revisions before developer hand-off.

    Takeaways

  • When designing a product,experience should come first. Otherwise, be prepared for more time and cost being spent on the project down the road.
  • To help users understand where they are and locate what they are searching for, be contextual,transparent, and approachable.
  • Always keep in mind that you are designing for people. People prefer simplicity and clarity. Sometimes something simple as a list rather than a description can be clearer.
