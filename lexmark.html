ServiceNow Redesign

ServiceNow is a customizable SaaS web app for Lexmark employees. On the platform, employees can submit request forms for any necessary services or jobs.

Team
UX Team Manager
UX Researcher
UX Design Intern (ahem, me!)

My Role
I conducted user research, led the design direction, built a fully functioning prototype, and prepared the documentation for developer hand-off. 🎉

Tools
Axure, Sketch

Duration
3 months

Overview

There was an employee consensus that ServiceNow created more problems than it solved. Luckily, the UX team had an intern to share with the ServiceNow team.

The outcome of my redesign was an enhanced usability score of a 36% improvement and a higher average of employee satisfaction ratings.

THE PROBLEM

People weren't using ServiceNow as much as they should

ServiceNow only received 12% of service requests. In the past 12 months, the phone lines received 20,000+ requests and were often busy.

Busy phone lines meant additional employees were required, and employees experienced longer wait times. ServiceNow ROI was abysmal.


ServiceNow Team




THE challenge

How might we improve the user experience of ServiceNow to help increase its engagement?

Interviews

There was ample opportunity to improve the platform

Baseline Usability Test

Employees were failing 47% of the time on ServiceNow

The interviews helped with problem validation, but we needed concrete data. I conducted usability tests with ten employees on the ten most common tasks to identify the exact problematic steps in the user flow.



Interface Pain Points

1
Unnecessary back & forth clicks

The IA did not reflect the employee's mental models. Unnecessary searching behavior was rampant.

2
Too much guesswork

The form titles in the dropdown were unclear. Employees weren't sure of what to expect.



Form Pain Points

3
Poor Content Hierarchy

The contents of the form were not digestable at a glance and the long forms were overwhelming.

4
Lack of Help

Users were left to fend for themselves when they were unsure of what the options were.

5
Error Prone

Options with either/or relationships involved checkboxes rather than radio buttons

Card Sort

When working on an internal tool, target users are close

We conducted open card sorts with Lexmark employees to create ServiceNow's new information architecture. With the new IA, we eliminated unnecessary searching behaviors and reduced browsing time.



Original Pain Points
Employees saw forms that weren't relevant to their location. It led to unnecessary clicks.




The Redesign
Employees no longer need to click back and forth within the IA. Every new level helps them become closer to their goal.

Wireframes

User needs weren't the only consideration

Initially, I explored different single-column formats for the form redesign. Unfortunately, the developer did not approve of the single-column designs because of the ServiceNow platform's technical limitations.


Initial Mockup

Mature UX teams already have their design system

The UX team already had its components library and spec documentation. While the design system saved me time from creating UI elements, it also meant I couldn't influence the visual language. At this point, my reasoning for updating the visuals for one section within the ServiceNow platform was weak and tabled.




Why Cards?

1
Easy to Scan

Cards divide information into meaningful sections and content hierarchy is clear.

2
More Surface Area

The previous dropdown interaction left no room to explain confusing form titles. Cards have ample room to provide users with helpful descriptions.





What's Changed?

1
Improved Content Hierarchy

Categorizing the content helps with providing context for unclear terms but also for improved scannability.

2
Proactive Error Prevention

Limiting user action to prevent errors. For example, using radio buttons for either/or relationships when previously, the forms employed checkboxes.

Using dropdowns for building number selection.



What's changed?
Previously, there were 8 input fields even when they weren't always necessary. For the redesign, there are two input fields that can be replicated as necessary


What's changed?
Previously, all the information was visible on load, but for the redesign, options will show depending on what the users click

Prototype

Fully functioning prototype on Axure

Final Usability Test

My design demonstrated a 36% improvement



After the final round of testing which included the same 10 tasks as before but conducted with 10 different employees, I made minor changes to strings to hopefully further increase the 89% success rate. I finished off the project with writing and turning in the documentation for developers.







Reflection

Ultimately, I became a better designer


1. Communicate often and early with developers.
Actually, the quality of communication matters too. Be specific with details of the design and be pointed with questions about limitations.

2. Buisness are important too
As a designer, we cannot always do what is best for our users. We need to be able to do what is best for our users given the resources that we have.

3. Use current components if there is any but be persuasive about updating it
I wish I had more time to help update our design system or even push for a separate style guide for internal tools than use the same one for consumer facing products.
