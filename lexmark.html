Overview

As a UX design intern, I redesigned ServiceNow, a customizable SaaS for technical management support for Lexmark employees.

There was a consensus among employees that ServiceNow created more problems than it solved.

As a UX design intern, I led a user-centered approach to redesign ServiceNow's information architecture, its interface, and its UX. Usability tests, card sorts, and interviews supported my design decisions.

The outcome of my redesign was an enhanced usability score of a 36% improvement and higher employee satisfaction ratings.

My role

I had the opportunity to conduct user research, iterate on my designs, and create a prototype on Axure. After my internship, I independently explored the platform's visual design and microinteractions.

THE PROBLEM

People weren't using ServiceNow as much as they should

Of all service requests, ServiceNow received only 12% of service requests. With the remaining tens of thousands of service requests coming in through phone calls, the phone lines were always busy and required extra employees. Employees calling in couldn't promptly do their actual work because they were experiencing longer wait times.

Baseline usability test

People were failing 47% of the time

Employees were failing almost once out of every two attempts to find the right form. The usability tests helped me pinpoint the exact steps in the user flow where users were making errors.

Card Sort

New information architecture

In the previous design, location selection happened at different points the hierarchy. Often times, employees sifted through multiple forms that did not even pertain to their location when trying to find the right forms. The new design prioritizes location first to cut out any unnecessary searching behaviors.

Ideation

Considerations

Forms do not always have to display all the information at first load. Make information appear based on what they select in the forms. Keep forms dynamic.



Prevent user error by keeping the information digestible at a glance and limiting user actions (e.g. using radio buttons rather than checkboxes will only allow users to select one option).



Build users' confidence in their decision making processes by giving them context such as helper texts and description. Adding text to a design does not automatically make it more complex.

Reflection

Ultimately, I became a better designer


1. Communicate often and early with developers.
Actually, the quality of communication matters too. Be specific with details of the design and be pointed with questions about limitations.

2. Buisness are important too
As a designer, we cannot always do what is best for our users. We need to be able to do what is best for our users given the resources that we have.

3. Use current components if there is any but be persuasive about updating it
I wish I had more time to help update our design system or even push for a separate style guide for internal tools than use the same one for consumer facing products.
