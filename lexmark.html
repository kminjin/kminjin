ServiceNow Redesign

ServiceNow is a customizable SaaS web app for Lexmark employees. Employees can submit request forms for any necessary services or jobs on the platform.

Team
UX Team Manager
UX Researcher
UX Design Intern (ahem, me!)

My Role
I conducted user research, led the design direction, built a fully functioning prototype, and prepared the documentation for developer hand-off.

Tools
Axure, Sketch

Duration
3 months

Overview

There was a consensus among employees that ServiceNow created more problems than it solved. Fortunately, the UX team had an intern to help improve the platform.

The outcome of my redesign was an enhanced usability score of a 36% improvement and employees reporting higher satisfaction ratings.

THE PROBLEM

People weren't using ServiceNow as much as they should

ServiceNow only received 12% of service requests. In the past 12 months, the phone lines received 20,000+ requests and were often busy.

Busy phone lines meant additional employees were required, and employees experienced longer wait times. ServiceNow ROI was abysmal!


ServiceNow Team

How might we improve the user experience of ServiceNow to help increase engagement?

Interviews

There was ample opportunity to improve the platform



Interface Pain Points

1 - Unnecessary back & forth clicks
The IA did not reflect employee's mental models and caused unnecessary additional searching behaviors.


2 - Too much guesswork
The form titles in the dropdown were unclear and employees weren't sure of what they were clicking on.



Form Pain Points

3 - Poor Content Hierarchy
The content of the form was not digestable at a glance.

4 - Lack of Help
Users were left to fend for themselves when they were unsure of what the options were.

5 - Error prone
Options with either/or relationships involved checkboxes rather than radio buttons

Baseline usability test

People were failing 47% of the time

Employees were failing almost once out of every two attempts to find the right form. The usability tests helped me pinpoint the exact steps in the user flow where users were making errors.

Test Details
53% Success Rate
10 Tasks
10 Target users

Card Sort

New information architecture

In the previous design, location selection happened at different points the hierarchy. Often times, employees sifted through multiple forms that did not even pertain to their location when trying to find the right forms. The new design prioritizes location first to cut out any unnecessary searching behaviors.

Ideation

Considerations

Forms do not always have to display all the information at first load. Make information appear based on what they select in the forms. Keep forms dynamic.



Prevent user error by keeping the information digestible at a glance and limiting user actions (e.g. using radio buttons rather than checkboxes will only allow users to select one option).



Build users' confidence in their decision making processes by giving them context such as helper texts and description. Adding text to a design does not automatically make it more complex.


Wireframes

Working with technical limitations

Initially, I was exploring different ways to design a single column form. There was plenty of research online that supported single column as best practice. However, when I was showing wireframes for a one column design to the developer, she stated we needed to stick with the original two column design format.

Mockup

Working with Lexmark's components library

Although I had my own vision for the visual design, I had to work with spec that already existed at Lexmark. ServiceNow was a huge platform and I was only working on the Facilities & Site section of the platform. The argument that this specific section needed to look completely different from the rest was a weak one. For the first mock up, I designed within Lexmark's design system.

Deep Dive

Content heavy screens does not have to have a bad UX



Change 1
Cards > Dropdown
Cards allow room for description texts for potentially confusing form titles such as "Badge Reader - Security Lexington".


Change 2
Improved hierarchy with categorization of content

Change 3
Error prevention: radio buttons for either/or relationships

Change 4
Helper text for confused users

Aha moment! Forms can be dynamic



What's changed?
Previously, there were 8 input fields even when they weren't always necessary. For the redesign, there are two input fields that can be replicated as necessary


What's changed?
Previously, all the information was visible on load, but for the redesign, options will show depending on what the users click

Final Usability Test

My design demonstrated a 36% improvement



After the final round of testing which included the same 10 tasks as before but conducted with 10 different employees, I made minor changes to strings to hopefully further increase the 89% success rate. I finished off the project with writing and turning in the documentation for developers.







Reflection

Ultimately, I became a better designer


1. Communicate often and early with developers.
Actually, the quality of communication matters too. Be specific with details of the design and be pointed with questions about limitations.

2. Buisness are important too
As a designer, we cannot always do what is best for our users. We need to be able to do what is best for our users given the resources that we have.

3. Use current components if there is any but be persuasive about updating it
I wish I had more time to help update our design system or even push for a separate style guide for internal tools than use the same one for consumer facing products.
