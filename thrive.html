Thrive

Helping people eat less meat

Role

Product Designer

Duration

5 months

Tools

Sketch, InVision

Type

Individual Side Project

Introduction

Initial Problem Discovery

As an environmental advocate, I've always sought ways to help our planet. While watching an environmental documentary titled Cowspiracy, I unexpectedly learned that the animal agriculture industry was the biggest contributor to climate change. To address this issue, I decided to help minimize the negative impact of the animal agriculture industry on our Earth, by exploring ways to help people eat less meat.


The Problem

How can I as a product designer, help people eat less meat? Who is my user group? What features are they looking for?


My Process

With this idea, there were many questions I needed answered. I knew assisting users to make such a significant lifestyle change such as quitting meat would not be a straight-forward problem. I needed to take special care in unravelling the complexities of human behavior and I felt methods such as surveys would lack when I was trying to understand the nuances of people's narratives.




The Solution

My solution was Thrive, an all-in-one mobile app that helps people eat less meat by providing users the positive encouragement, the access to social support, and the tools they need to succeed.


The Research

Interview Findings

I wanted to learn about how vegans became vegans, what obstacles non-vegans experienced when trying to become vegan, and what current struggles some vegans faced. I conducted 10 semi-structured interviews with 3 vegans and 7 omnivores.




The Research

Competitive Analysis Findings

After identifying the obstacles users needed to overcome, I wanted to see if there was currently a solution in the market that addressed all four obstacles. I examined three mobile apps that aimed to help people eat less meat.


Identifying Design Opportunities

Persona

Taking insights from the interviews, I created 3 personas that represented the archetypes of the user group. Throughout the process, I turned back to the personas to make sure every design decision was looping back to user needs.

Identifying Design Opportunities

Journey Maps

I then mapped out a journey map for each of my personas detailing their actions throughout the day and the potential emotions involved. The journey maps were extremely helpful in helping me visualize the personas' areas of frustration and create focal points in the problem space.

Ideation

Diverging

Clearly, helping people eliminate meat from their diet was not going to be an easy task. I tried to brainstorm as many ideas as I can during the ideation phase.

Ideation

Converging

After considering all the possibilities, I did a pros & cons list for each feature idea and narrowed it down to four features.

Ideation

User Flow

Working out the user flow helped me get a rough idea about how I wanted to map out Thrive from onboarding to finish.


Refining

Paper Prototype

I then worked on paper prototypes to get quick feedback from users before starting the wireframes. There were couple interactions such as graph viewing that users did not find particularly helpful and those features were eliminated for the wireframes.


Refining

Wireframes

The wireframes were again used during another quick user feedback session.

Refining

First Prototype

There were a lot of feedback I received from a thorough usability testing of the first prototype. People loved the idea, but felt the visuals could be improved. To my surprise, one user stated that they were confused as to what the image in home was representing so I knew for the second prototype, I had to work out those issues.

Solution

Second Prototype

Most notable change was from the first and second prototype was how my weekly goal illustration was displayed in the Achievements screen. In the previous usability testing, users reported that they could not tell what stage of the goal they were in so for the second prototype, I included a day by day goal indicator. I also removed the "education" section because it worked much better in theory than in reality.

Reflection

What I learned

1. Technology only amplifies underlying human forces. So study people, learn about people, and listen to people.For technology to actually work, the right intent and capacity have to be there first.
2. When designing for an app that is meant to encourage a lifestyle change, it does not mean I have to include a lot of features. Always consider the bare necessities for a successful product(the mvp).

Reflection

Where to Improve

Push myself further in visual design by thinking about the affordances on and transitions between the screens.
