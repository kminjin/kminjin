Overview

As a UX design intern, I redesigned ServiceNow, a customizable SaaS for technical management support for Lexmark employees.

There was a consensus among employees that ServiceNow created more problems than it solved. Employees actively avoided the web app.

As a UX design intern, I led a user-centered approach to redesign ServiceNow's information architecture, its interface, and improve its UX. Usability tests, card sorts, and interviews supported my design decisions.

The outcome of my redesign was an enhanced usability score of a 36% improvement and higher employee satisfaction ratings.

THE PROBLEM

People weren't using ServiceNow as much as they should

Of all service requests, ServiceNow received only 12% of service requests. With the remaining tens of thousands of service requests coming in through phone calls, the phone lines were always busy and required extra employees. Employees calling in couldn't promptly do their actual work because they were experiencing longer wait times.



ServiceNow Team

Challenge

Increase the usage of ServiceNow

I hoped that by improving the user experience of ServiceNow, employee opinions on the platform will because positive, and in result, usage of platform will increase. I not only needed to understand why people were avoiding the platform but also be able to pinpoint the exact steps that were frustrating the employees. Before I get into the process however, here is quick glance at the solution.

Pain Points at Glance

There was ample opportunity to improve the platform



1. Unclear Writing
The form titles were confusing. Employees weren't sure that they were submitting the correct request form.

2. Poor Information Architecture
The IA did not reflect employee's mental models and caused unnecessary additional searching behaviors.

3. Overwhelming Forms
The forms were long and had so many opportunities for users to make errors.

Solution at glance

Success rate went from 53% to 89%



1. New Information Architecture
A user begins with location and then only sees forms relevant to their location.

2. Cards > Dropdown
Cards allowed room for description texts for potentially confusing form titles.

3. 10 redesigned request forms
Forms were made to be dynamic to help users see only the relevant information pertaining to their request

While watching an environmental documentary titled Cowspiracy, I unexpectedly learned that the animal agriculture industry was a significant contributor to climate change. With this newfound knowledge, I decided to say goodbye to my meat-rich diet and embrace vegetarianism. However, I couldn't give up the love of my life- chicken chalupas, and hence, a new project emerged.

The solution

Behavioral change can be fun and rewarding

My solution is Thrive, an all-in-one mobile app that helps people eat less meat by providing them with positive encouragement, social support, and the necessary tools such as vegan recipes.


Interviews

People have different motivations but similar challenges

Designers design for others and so, it was time to learn from the people. I opted for interviews over surveys because I needed to deeply explore people's narratives. From 10 semi-structured interviews, I identified the motivations behind veganism, the struggles vegans faced, and the obstacles for non-vegans.




Competitive Analysis

Current products weren't solving the problem

My goal was to discover if there was a solution that addressed all four interview insights. Examining the pro-veganism apps in the market showed me that the problem space didn't have an all-in-one solution yet.


Personas

Who are my users?


1. The Proactive Mother
Julianne is a mom to two young boys with asthma. She no longer wants to support the industry responsible for 18% of all harmful gases.

2. The Young Animal Activist
Beth, an activist for PETA, has been vegan since she was 15. The number of animals she has saved thus far motivates her.

3. The Health Conscious Athlete
Mark is a boxer who is weary of heart disease and has to quit eating meat. He would love to connect with others on a similar journey.

Journey Map

My target group isn't equipped with any necessary tools


The journey map for my persona Julianne (The Proactive Mother), further supported the existence of two main problems discovered in the interviews and helped me identify design opportunities-

1. Target group's lack of experience with vegan recipes
2. Inability to connect with others who are also trying to eat less meat

content strategy

Building a well-integrated and logical flow

After determining the final features, I iterated on a flow that required the least amount of user input. I chose to use a bottom nav over a hamburger menu to help users move quickly.


A/B TESTS of wireframes

When in doubt? Turn to users

I created wireframes of the "Achievements" feature and ran A/B tests. The A/B tests helped me figure out what users preferred in terms of the ease of digesting the content, entering data, and visual appeal.


The Winner?

Scrolling

Scrolling allowed for better information visibility and content digestion.







The Winner?

Smaller FAB

The FAB was familiar to users and it took up less screen space.

Iterating on feedback

Bringing more clarity with every iteration

There were many other iterations not shown here. From wireframes to v1, I made design decisions that can account for uncommon use cases such as adding or deleting a group. From v1 to v2, I decided to improve the IA and increase the prominence of the search bar to promote exploratory behavior. Lastly, following existing design systems and designing on an 8pt grid tremendously helped the interface design.






Style Guide

Being intentional with every design decision


1. Typography
Typeface needed to work headers, body copy, and captions. I chose the classic and accessible Source Sans Pro for optimal legibility.

2. UI Buttons
I learned that users weren't comfortable with my newly created patterns. I ultimately turned to Google's Material Design.

3. Color
I wanted a white background to make sure the content is easy to read. For the UI, I opted for a bright orange and a complementary teal.

Solution

FEATURE I
VISUALIZATION OF PROGRESS

Users can daily log their meat-free days throughout the week and be encouraged by the visual representation of the improving environment.

Users can track their progress and see the positive impact of their accumulated meat-free days.










FEATURE II
FRIENDLY MOTIVATION

Users can form groups with people who have similar goals and compare how they rank to their peers in the number of meat-free days.










FEATURE III
TOOLS FOR SUCCESS

Users can look up recipes or learn about vegan diets to equip themselves on this journey.








Reflection

Ultimately, I became a better designer


1. DESIGN SYSTEMS. DESIGN SYSTEMS. DESIGN SYSTEMS.
I fell in love with the IOS human interface & Google's material design guidelines. Existing systems allow me to use my brainpower on other more pressing design decisions.

2. Think in product & not features
Originally, I had about six features I wanted to include. But feedback showed that having all six features was extremely overwhelming. I learned to ground myself in users' problems and the product's goals.

3. Even as a product designer, visual design skills are important
With great visual design, users are willing to take the time to learn even the most complex products. Great visual design is undeniably a part of what makes a great UX. Every design decision needs to be intentional.
