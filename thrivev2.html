Thrive

Thrive is a mobile app-based solution that helps people eat less meat

Role

Product Designer

Duration

5 months

Tools

Sketch, InVision,
Currently tinkering w/ Principle

Type

Individual Side Project

context

I needed help quitting Chicken Chalupas from Taco Bell

While watching an environmental documentary titled Cowspiracy, I unexpectedly learned that the animal agriculture industry was a significant contributor to climate change. With this newfound knowledge, I decided to say goodbye to my meat-rich diet and embrace vegetarianism. However, I couldn't give up the love of my life- chicken chalupas, and hence, a new project emerged.

The problem

Turns out, my mom wants to eat less meat too

Over the last several years, there has been a continuous rise in vegetarian and vegan diets. I found others like me, who also struggled to limit their meat intake. I decided to explore how I can, as a product designer, help people eat less meat.

The solution

Behavioral change can be fun and rewarding

My solution is Thrive, an all-in-one mobile app that helps people eat less meat by providing them with positive encouragement, social support, and the necessary tools such as vegan recipes.


Interviews

People have different motivations but similar challenges

Designers design for others and so, it was time to learn from the people. I opted for interviews over surveys because I needed to deeply explore people's narratives. From 10 semi-structured interviews, I identified the motivations behind veganism, the struggles vegans faced, and the obstacles for non-vegans.




Competitive Analysis

Current products weren't solving the problem

My goal was to discover if there was a solution that addressed all four interview insights. Examining the pro-veganism apps in the market showed me that the problem space didn't have an all-in-one solution yet.


Personas

Who are my users?


1. The Proactive Mother
Julianne is a mom to two young boys with asthma. She no longer wants to support the industry responsible for 18% of all harmful gases.

2. The Young Animal Activist
Beth, an activist for PETA, has been vegan since she was 15. The number of animals she has saved thus far motivates her.

3. The Health Conscious Athlete
Mark is a boxer who is weary of heart disease and has to quit eating meat. He would love to connect with others on a similar journey.

Journey Map

My target group isn't equipped with any necessary tools


The journey map for my persona Julianne (The Proactive Mother), further supported the existence of two main problems discovered in the interviews and helped me identify design opportunities-

1. Target group's lack of experience with vegan recipes
2. Inability to connect with others who are also trying to eat less meat

Ideation

The more users turn to Thrive, the more effective it will be

"Rewarding, enticing, and resourceful" were themes I considered while ideating. Above all else, my app needed to be convenient- users should not experience a waiting time when inputting or expecting data.


Make it a mobile app 📱

Every persona was a busy person on-the-move. The solution needed to be readily accessible.

Reward users 🏆

Incorporate weekly "meat-free" goals and gamify it. Gamification allows for quick feedback.

Be resourceful 🥕

Provide vegan recipes! People want to eat vegan meals besides PB & J.

Motivate Users 💯

Interviewees stated it is hard to see the fruit of their labor after having gone meat-free, so include metrics about users' positive impact.

Provide social support 🧑‍🤝‍🧑

Connect users with others who are also trying to eat less meat and allow them to engage in friendly competition on leaderboards.

content strategy

Building a well-integrated and logical flow

After determining the final features, I iterated on a flow that required the least amount of user input. I chose to use a bottom nav over a hamburger menu to help users move quickly.


A/B TESTS of wireframes

When in doubt? Turn to users

I created wireframes of the "Achievements" feature and ran A/B tests. The A/B tests helped me figure out what users preferred in terms of the ease of digesting the content, entering data, and visual appeal.


The Winner?

Users preferred to scroll.

I went forward with option B because scrolling allowed for better information visibility and content digestion.







The Winner?

Users preferred the smaller FAB.

I went ahead with option B because users are more familiar with the smaller FAB when compared to the sticky button. The smaller FAB also took up less precious screen space.

Iterating on feedback

Bringing more clarity with every iteration

I received feedback from potential users and a designer I connected with on LinkedIn. Following existing design systems such as Google's material guidelines and designing on a 8pt grid tremendously helped.






Style Guide

Being intentional with every design decision


1. Typography
Typeface needed to work headers, body copy, and captions. I chose the classic and accessible Source Sans Pro for optimal legibility.

2. UI Buttons
I learned that users weren't comfortable with my newly created patterns. I ultimately turned to Google's Material Design.

3. Color
I wanted a white background to make sure the content is easy to read. For the UI, I opted for a bright orange and a complementary teal.

Solution

FEATURE I
VISUALIZATION OF PROGRESS

Users can daily log their meat-free days throughout the week and be encouraged by the visual representation of the environment improving.

Users are able to track their progress and see the positive impact of their accumulated meat-free days.










FEATURE II
FRIENDLY MOTIVATION

Users can form groups with people who have similar goals and compare how they rank to their peers in number of meat-free days.










FEATURE III
TOOLS FOR SUCCESS

Users can look up recipes or learn about vegan diets to equip themselves on this journey.








Reflection

Ultimately, I became a better designer

1. DESIGN SYSTEMS. DESIGN SYSTEMS. DESIGN SYSTEMS.
I fell in love with the IOS human interface & Google's material design guidelines. Existing systems allow me to use my brain power on other more pressing design decisions.

2. Think in product & not features
Originally, I had about about 6 features I wanted to include. But feedback showed that having all 6 features was extremely overwhelming. I learned to ground myself in users' problems and the product's goals.

3. As designers, we should listen to our users but not blindly allow them to tell us what to do. We need to interpret our findings from user research and make sense of them before supporting out design decisions.
