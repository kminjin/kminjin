Thrive

Thrive is a mobile app-based solution that helps people eat less meat

Role

Product Designer

Duration

5 months

Tools

Sketch, InVision,
Currently tinkering w/ Principle

Type

Individual Side Project

context

I couldn't quit Chicken Chalupas from Taco Bell

While watching an environmental documentary titled Cowspiracy, I unexpectedly learned that the animal agriculture industry was the biggest contributor to climate change. With this newfound knowledge, I decided to say goodbye to my meat-rich diet and embrace vegeterianism. However, I couldn't give up the love of my life- chicken chalupas, and hence, a new project emerged.

The problem

Turns out, my mom wants to eat less meat too

Over the last several years, there has been a continuous rise in vegeterian and vegan diets. I found others, like me, who also struggled to limit their meat-intake. I decided to explore, how I can, as a product designer, help people eat less meat.

The solution

Make behavioral change rewarding, fun, and valuable

My solution is Thrive, an all-in-one mobile app that helps people eat less meat by providing them with the positive encouragement, social support, and the tools needed to succeed.


Interviews

People have different motivations but similar challenges

Designers design for others and so, it was time to learn from the people. I opted for interviews over surveys because I needed to deeply explore people's narratives. From 10 semi-structured interviews, I identified the motivations behind veganism, the struggles vegans faced, and the obstacles for non-vegans.




Competitive Analysis

Current products weren't solving the problem

My goal was to discover if there was a solution that addressed all 4 interview insights. Examining the pro-veganism apps in the market showed me that the problem space didn't have an all-in-one solution yet.


Personas

Who are the users?


1. The Proactive Mother
She no longer wants to support the industry responsible for 18% of all harmful gases. Her two sons both have asthma.

2. The Young Animal Activist
Beth works for PETA and she's been vegan since she was 15. The amount of animals she has saved thus far, motivate her.

3. The Health Conscious Athlete
Mark is a boxer who is weary of heart disease. He has a competitive spirit and would love to connect with others in a similar journey.

Journey Map

Solution needs to be a source for resources


The journey map for my persona Julianne (The Proactive Mother), further supported the existence of two main problems discovered in the interviews and helped me identify design opportunities-

1. Target group's lack of experience with vegan recipes
2. Inability to connect with others who are also trying to eat less meat

Ideation

The more users turn to Thrive, the more effective it will be

After understanding the needs of my target group, I generated a series of themes to consider while ideating: rewarding and enticing, resourceful but above all else, be convenient. Users should not experience a waiting time when they are inputting or expecting data

Make it a mobile app

All three personas were busy people on-the-move. Solution needed to be readily and easily accessible.

How can Thrive reward its users?

Include weekly "meat-free day" goal and incorporate a gamified way of displaying progress. Gamification gives people quick feedback and allows them to see their performance level at a glance.

How can Thrive motivate its users?

Interviewees consistently mentioned that it is hard to see the difference on the world even after having gone meat-free, so include concrete metrics about their positive impact.

What do Thrive users need?

Vegan recipes! People want to eat vegan meals besides PB & J.

What about social support?

Allow Thrive to be a platform where user can form groups with people who are all trying to eat less meat and engage in friendly competition through leaderboards.

A/B TESTS of wireframes

When in doubt? Turn to users

I created wireframes of the "Achievements" feature and ran A/B tests. The A/B tests helped me figure out what users preferred in terms of the ease of digesting the content, entering data, and visual appeal.


Tabs VS Scroll

Users preferred to scroll over tabbing.

I went forward with option b because scrolling helped with information visibility and made it easier for users to digest the content.







Sticky VS Floating

Users preferred the smaller FAB over the larger, sticky button.

I went ahead with option B because users are more familiar with the smaller FAB and it also took up less screen space.

Iterating on feedback

Bringing more clarity with every iteration

I received feedback from potential users and a senior designer I connected with on LinkedIn. With each iteration, the experience was simplified. Most notably, following existing design systems such as Google's material design guidelines and designing on a 8pt grid system helped the UI tremendously.






Style Guide

Being intentional with every design decision


1. Typography
I wanted to work with a typeface that can be used for headers, body copy, and captions. I went with the classic and accedssible Source Sans Pro for optimal legibility.

2. UI Buttons
Originally, I experimented with creating entirely new UI components but learned early on that users weren't comfortable with my patterns. I ultimately turned to Google Design Material Guidelines.

3. Color
I wanted my app to have a white background to make sure the content is easy to read. For the UI, I opted for a bright orange for the most important action button and a complementary teal for the rest.

Solution

Final Solution

FEATURE I
VISUALIZATION OF PROGRESS

Users can daily log their meat-free days throughout the week and be encouraged by the visual representation of the environment improving.

Users are able to track their progress and see the positive impact of their accumulated meat-free days.










FEATURE II
FRIENDLY MOTIVATION

Users can form groups with people who have similar goals and compare how they rank to their peers in numnber of meat-free days.










FEATURE III
TOOLS FOR SUCCESS

Users can look up recipes or learn about vegan diets to equip themselves on this journey.








Reflection

What I learned

1. Technology only amplifies underlying human forces. So study people, learn about people, and listen to people. For technology to actually work, the right intent and capacity have to be there first.

2. When designing for an app that is meant to encourage a lifestyle change, it does not mean I have to include a lot of features. Always consider the bare necessities for a successful product(the mvp).

3. As designers, we should listen to our users but not blindly allow them to tell us what to do. We need to interpret our findings from user research and make sense of them before supporting out design decisions.

Reflection

Where to Improve

Push myself further in visual design by thinking about the affordances on and transitions between the screens.
