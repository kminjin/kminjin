Helping people eat less meat

Introduction

The Problem

As an environmental advocate, I've always sought ways to help our planet. While watching an environmental documentary titled Cowspiracy, I unexpectedly learned that the animal agriculture industry was the biggest contributor to climate change. To address this issue, I decided to explore ways to help people eat less meat.

How can I as a product designer, help people eat less meat?
Who is my user group?
What features are they looking for?


The Solution

My solution was Thrive, an all-in-one mobile app that helps people eat less meat by providing users the positive encouragement, the access to social support, and the tools they need to succeed.


The Research

User Interviews (10) & Key Insights

My goal was to gather information about the struggles vegans faced when it came to maintaining their diet, the obstacles non-vegans saw in their journey to become vegan, and the different motivations behind veganism.




Competitive Analysis Discovery

My goal was to discover if there were still room for a solution that addressed all 4 interview insights. Examining the pro-veganism apps in the market showed me that the problem space didn't have an all-in-one solution yet.


Discovering Design Opportunities

Personas

With the interview data, I created 3 personas: 1 vegan and 2 omnivores who wanted to become vegan for different reasons. These personas ensured that every design decision looped back to meet user needs.


Journey Maps

The journey map for my persona Julianne, further supported the existence of many problems discovered in the interviews: (1) lack of experience with vegan recipes and (2) inability to connect with people who are vegan.


Ideation

Product requirements

After understanding the needs of my target group, I generated a series of themes to consider while ideating. I determined that the more the users turn to my solution, the more effective my solution will be.

Design

The Experience & User Flow


After reviewing the product requirements, I decided my solution would be a mobile app due to its convenience for daily use. I created scenarios (image 2) detailing 3 key interactions between my app and its users. Then, I created a user flow to form the backbone of the app (image 1).


Refining

First Prototype

There were a lot of feedback I received from a thorough usability testing of the first prototype. People loved the idea, but felt the visuals could be improved. To my surprise, one user stated that they were confused as to what the image in home was representing so I knew for the second prototype, I had to work out those issues.

Solution

Second Prototype

Most notable change from the first and second prototype was how my weekly goal illustration was displayed in the Achievements screen. In the previous usability testing, users reported that they could not tell what stage of the goal they were in because there was just an image and a "plus" button which gave them no context. So for the second prototype, I included a day by day goal indicator.

Reflection

What I learned

1. Technology only amplifies underlying human forces. So study people, learn about people, and listen to people. For technology to actually work, the right intent and capacity have to be there first.
2. When designing for an app that is meant to encourage a lifestyle change, it does not mean I have to include a lot of features. Always consider the bare necessities for a successful product(the mvp).
3. As designers, we should listen to our users but not blindly allow them to tell us what to do. We need to interpret our findings from user research and make sense of them before supporting out design decisions.

Reflection

Where to Improve

Push myself further in visual design by thinking about the affordances on and transitions between the screens.
