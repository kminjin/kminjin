Project Context

As an intern, I independently led a user-centered design project to redesign ServiceNow, an internal service tool for Lexmark employees to submit requests for any items or services they need. I supported design decisions with user research and increased the goal success rate among the 10 most common tasks on the platform by 36%.










Why did ServiceNow need to be redesigned?




Manager

"I rather place my requests over the phone because the forms online are extensive and ask for so much detail"

Administrative Assistant

"I would have to wait weeks before being notified I submitted the wrong form. It is hard to tell the purpose of the forms"

ServiceNow Team

"Trying to redirect wrong forms take up a lot of our team's time... and the phone calls are overwhelming"

    • SOLUTIONS AT GLANCE

    Between the old and new design, I saw a 36% overall increase in success rates among 10 common tasks on ServiceNow. I achieved this improvement by changing the information architecture and redesigning the request forms. Being able to support my design decisions with an understanding of employees' needs, frustrations, and existing mental models led to drastic changes.

    Design Changes

    1. The new design allows employees to begin with their location and the system only displays the relevant requests forms for the selected location.

    2. The drop down interaction for form selection is replaced with cards that have description for each form.




    Example of Improved Form Design


    Previous Design


    New Design


    The Changes

    1. Employees did not know what "Amia" or "Leap" meant. The new design has help icons for more information.

    2. Standard cube sizing was not consisntent in the old design. The new design instead asks a separate question entirely, and even gives employees the option to ask for help.

    3. An employee can choose either a single monitor or dual monitor but not both. The new design no longer has checkboxes and instead uses radio buttons to prevent multiple options being selected.

    • USER TESTING

    Because there was hardly any data, I wanted to gather my own. Although specific quantitative data such as bounce rates would have been useful, I had limited time at Lexmark. With the limited time available, I decided to do user testing and collect success rates on 10 most common tasks.

      1. After work-hours, items from people’s cubicles are disappearing. The group decides to install security cameras. Please submit a request.
      2. You have lower back pain that prevents you from sitting for more than 15 minutes straight. Please submit a request for a stand-up desk.
      3. You are a manager who wants to break the wall between two small meeting rooms and make it into one medium sized meeting room. Please submit a request.
      4. A projector is broken. Please submit a request.
      5. You need to ship a 1000 lb. equipment to China. Please submit a request.
      6. You recently got injured and will need to use a wheelchair for several months. The entry to your office does not have an automotive door, please submit a request.
      7. In your office, there is a 15ft x 5ft sized whiteboard hung up. It needs to be relocated into a different room. Please submit a request.
      8. You want to move a couch out of your office. Please submit a request.
      9. A cabinet has been in your office for the last ten years. You want to get it opened, but it is locked. Please submit a request
      10. You have highly sensitive documents and you need a padlock for your door. Please submit a request.






    Phase 2 of 3: Explore

    FIRST DESIGN CONCEPT

    Example pain points in old design & design decisions to address them

    01

    Previous Design

    When requesting a security camera,"Site Operations Security Request" seems like a great option, until you click on the form and realize it's only for location in Zhuhai, China.

    New Design

    Came up with a design that prioritizes location first to cut out any unnecessary searching behaviors.

    02

    Previous Design

    For an ergonomic furniture request, there is a list of options to select however, not everyone knows what each option means and options that are either/or are checkboxes which allow for multiple selections.

    New Design

    Make the information digestible, offer categories, use radio buttons rather than checkboxes, and offer help where needed with help icons.

    03

    Previous Design

    For the overall request selection process, the titles for each request were not clear or sometimes not representative of the request forms. For example, "Badge Reader-Security Lexington" request had more options besides a badge reader, but an employee would not know that from the current design.

    New Design

    Came up with a design that had helper text(right image #3) for each request form.





    In the new design, the most complex form can be submitted in four clicks rather than seven.
